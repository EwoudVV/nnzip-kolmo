"You're late," she said, without looking up from her newspaper.

"The train was held at the bridge. Some kind of signal failure."

"That's the third time this month."

"I know."

He hung his coat on the hook by the door and stood for a moment, deciding whether to mention the rain or let it go. The smell of garlic and onions drifted from the kitchen.

"Did you eat?" he asked.

"Hours ago. There's a plate in the oven."

"You didn't have to."

"I know I didn't have to." She turned a page. "It's there if you want it."

He went into the kitchen and pulled the plate out with a dish towel. Pasta, the simple kind she made when she was tired. He carried it to the table and sat across from her, and for a while there was only the sound of his fork against the plate and the rustle of newspaper.

"How was your day?" he tried.

"Fine."

"Anything happen?"

"No."

He nodded, and ate. He had learned, over the years, when to push and when not to. Tonight was not a pushing night. Outside, a car passed slowly through the puddles in the street, and somewhere down the block a dog barked twice and then was quiet.

"I saw your sister today," she said, after a long pause.

"Oh?"

"She asked about you."

"What did you tell her?"

"I told her you were fine."

He put his fork down. "Am I?"

She looked up from the newspaper for the first time since he had walked in. Her face was tired and her eyes were soft, and he could not tell whether she was about to smile or about to cry. She held his gaze for what felt like a long time.

"I don't know," she said. "Are you?"

He thought about it. The rain had started again outside, fat slow drops against the kitchen window, and the radiator clicked the way it always did when the temperature dropped.

"I don't know either," he said.

She folded the newspaper carefully and set it aside.

"Well," she said. "At least we know that much."
