The coffee shop on the corner had been there for as long as anyone in the neighborhood could remember. Its windows fogged up every morning when the espresso machine hissed to life, and the smell of freshly ground beans drifted out into the street whenever the door swung open. Regulars came and went with quiet familiarity. The barista, a woman in her thirties with ink on her fingers and a notebook always within reach, knew most of them by their orders before she knew them by name. There was the man who took his coffee black and sat by the window every weekday morning, reading the same battered paperback for what must have been the tenth time. There was the woman who arrived just after the school run, her hair still damp from the rain, who always ordered a flat white and a slice of banana bread. There were the students who lingered for hours with a single cortado between them, their laptops casting blue light across the wooden tables.

On a Tuesday in late October, a stranger walked in. He was perhaps sixty, with a neatly trimmed beard and a coat that looked expensive but worn. He stood at the counter for a long moment, studying the chalkboard menu as if he were reading a foreign language. When he finally spoke, his voice was softer than the barista expected, and he asked for a pour-over with a precision that suggested he had thought about it for some time. He paid in cash, exact change, and took a seat in the corner where the light was poor and the chairs were uncomfortable. He did not open a book or a phone. He simply sat, hands wrapped around the warm ceramic, and watched the street through the window. The barista wiped down the counter and tried not to look at him too often, but something about the stillness of the man made her aware of every movement she made.

Two hours later, he was still there, his coffee long since finished. He had not asked for a refill. He had not spoken to anyone. When the afternoon rush began and the line stretched out the door, he stood up, placed the empty cup carefully on the counter, nodded to the barista, and walked out into the gray light. She watched him cross the street and disappear around the corner, and she found herself wondering, in a way she had not wondered about anyone in years, whether he would ever come back.
