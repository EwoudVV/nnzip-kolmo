The honeybee, Apis mellifera, is a social insect that lives in colonies of up to sixty thousand individuals. A single colony consists of one queen, several hundred male drones, and tens of thousands of female worker bees. The queen is the only reproductive female in the colony and can lay up to two thousand eggs per day during the peak season. Worker bees perform a variety of tasks depending on their age, beginning with cleaning duties shortly after emerging from their cells and progressing through nursing, building, guarding, and finally foraging during the last weeks of their roughly six-week summer lifespan.

Foraging bees communicate the location of food sources to their hive mates through a behavior known as the waggle dance, first decoded by the Austrian biologist Karl von Frisch in the 1940s. A returning forager performs a figure-eight pattern on the vertical surface of the comb, and the angle of the central straight portion of the dance relative to vertical encodes the direction of the food source relative to the sun. The duration of the waggle phase encodes the distance, with longer waggles indicating more distant sources. Other workers in the hive follow the dancer closely and use the encoded information to fly directly to the indicated location, often several kilometers from the hive.

Honeybees produce honey by collecting nectar from flowers, storing it in a specialized stomach, and regurgitating it back at the hive where it is passed mouth to mouth among workers. Enzymes added during this process break down complex sugars into simpler ones, and the resulting liquid is deposited into wax cells where workers fan it with their wings to evaporate excess water. When the moisture content drops below about eighteen percent, the cell is sealed with a thin wax cap and the honey can be stored indefinitely without spoiling. A healthy colony in a temperate climate produces between twenty and sixty kilograms of surplus honey per year, far more than it needs to survive the winter.

The decline of honeybee populations in recent decades has been attributed to a combination of factors including habitat loss, pesticide exposure, parasitic mites, viral and fungal diseases, and the stress of long-distance transport for commercial pollination services. The phenomenon known as colony collapse disorder, in which the majority of worker bees abandon a hive containing a queen and ample food stores, was first widely reported in the United States in 2006 and remains incompletely understood.
